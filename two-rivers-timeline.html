The Four Separations

The work of separation in Abraham's life — Ch. 13, Abraham Park

"In reality, the history of Israel began with God's command for separation... The work of separation, however, is always accompanied by pain. It cannot be carried out or even understood without painful tears." — Abraham Park

I
First Separation · Genesis 12:1 · Acts 7:2–4
From his homeland & Terah
Ur of the Chaldeans → Haran → Canaan
▼
Ur of the Chaldeans was the center of ancient civilization — and the center of idolatry. Terah worshipped idols there. God commanded Abraham: "Go forth from your country, and from your relatives and from your father's house."

Abraham first left with Terah — but Terah stopped in Haran. God called again at age 75: leave your father's house. Abraham obeyed — though Terah was still alive, 145 years old. Terah was "as good as dead to him" (Acts 7:4 — the Greek word denotes symbolic death).

This first separation represents: separation from inherited identity — family, culture, religion received without personal examination before God.
The Pain
Leaving a 145-year-old father behind. In a patriarchal society, the firstborn in charge of the household — walking away. "It must have been heartbreaking." (Park)
A Question for You

What have you inherited from your family, culture, or religious tradition that you have never personally examined before God — that you assumed was right simply because it was always there?

"By faith Abraham obeyed when he was called to go out to a place that he was to receive as an inheritance. And he went out, not knowing where he was going." Hebrews 11:8
II
Second Separation · Genesis 13
From Lot
The Jordan Valley — "well-watered everywhere, like the garden of the Lord"
▼
Lot was not an enemy. He was family, a fellow traveler, a man of faith — Peter calls him "righteous Lot" (2 Pet 2:7-8). He had walked with Abraham through the hardest miles from Ur to Canaan.

But when they needed to separate, Lot "lifted up his eyes and saw all the valley of the Jordan, that it was well-watered everywhere." He chose the best land — and moved his tents toward Sodom.

This second separation represents: separation from those who compromise within the community of faith — beloved fellow believers whose eyes are always toward the well-watered valley.
The Pain
Separating from someone who was faithful in the hardest times. Not leaving the obviously wicked — but leaving the "good enough." This is harder, because love is real.
A Question for You

Who is the "Lot" in your life — someone you love, a fellow believer, whose vision consistently draws you toward the well-watered valley rather than toward God's actual call on your life?

"Abram settled in the land of Canaan, while Lot settled in the cities of the valley, and moved his tents as far as Sodom." Genesis 13:12
III
Third Separation · Genesis 21
From Ishmael
"Cast out the bondwoman and her son" — Galatians 4:30
▼
Ishmael was Abraham's firstborn son — 17 years of love, investment, and hope. He was born not from sin, but from Abraham's own impatience: human scheming when God's promise seemed too slow.

When Isaac was born, God confirmed: send Ishmael away. "The matter distressed Abraham greatly because of his son" (Gen 21:11). He loved Ishmael. But he obeyed.

This third separation represents: separation from what we built with our own hands — our plans, programs, strategies put in motion without waiting for God, that we love because we made them.
The Pain
"The pain of having his flesh torn away." (Park) Seventeen years of fatherhood. Walking Ishmael and Hagar to the door. Not releasing something evil — releasing something good that was not God's way.
A Question for You

What is the "Ishmael" you are holding — something you built, a plan you set in motion — that you know in your heart was not God's timing or way, but is too precious to release?

"Cast out the bondwoman and her son, for the son of the bondwoman shall not be an heir with the son of the free woman." Galatians 4:30
IV
Fourth Separation · Genesis 22
From Isaac
"Your son, your only son, whom you love" — Genesis 22:2
▼
Isaac was not Abraham's plan — he was God's own promise, the covenant son born to a 100-year-old man. He was purely gift, purely grace. And God said: offer him as a burnt offering.

"This separation was equivalent to death; it shook the very foundation of his existence." (Park) The test is not: can you give up your sin? But: can you return to God even what God gave you?

This fourth separation represents: the maturity of faith — returning to God what belongs to Him, even the blessings He gave. The 24 elders cast their crowns before the throne (Rev 4:10-11) — the same confession.
The Pain
Not releasing sin — releasing grace itself. The covenantal son. The sole object of his affection. "The pain of having the very foundation of his existence shaken." This is the deepest separation.
A Question for You

What is the "Isaac" in your life — a genuine blessing from God that you now hold so tightly it has become more central than God Himself? What would it mean to lay it on the altar?

"By Myself I have sworn, declares the Lord, because you have done this thing and have not withheld your son, your only son, indeed I will greatly bless you." Genesis 22:16–17
The Hidden Pattern
Each separation opened a deeper covenant:
After separation from Terah → Promise of land and descendants (Gen 12)
After separation from Lot → Land expanded as far as the eye can see (Gen 13)
After separation from Ishmael → Covenant renewed, Isaac confirmed (Gen 21)
After separation from Isaac → God swears by Himself — incredible blessing (Gen 22)
The depth of fellowship with God corresponds to the depth of separation from the world. Every separation makes room. God does not fill what is still full of something else.

Where are you in the four separations?

Not a test. An invitation.
